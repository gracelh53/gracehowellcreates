This website discusses the very real death of Paul McCartney in 1966. On his way back from a recording session of Sgt. Pepper Lonely Hearts Club Band he was in a horrific car accident which killed him. Furious with the studio kerfuffle, he floored it, went off into the night in a fit of rage and was killed in a car crash, some even saying he was gruesomely scalped during the crash. With the band floundering amid their pop peak, improbably, McCartney was then replaced by a lookalike called either William Shears Cambell or William Sheppard

Luckily, he was a man who could not only look like Macca but sing and write songs like him too — quite the find. The Beatles had just begun their musical career in 1963 with their album “Please Please Me” and were becoming very popular all over the world. The first time the rumors of McCartney’s apparent death were given any real credibility in a public sphere was on Sept. 17th, 1969, when Tim Harper wrote a piece referencing the rumor in the Times-Delphic, a campus newspaper for Drake University in Des Moines, Iowa. Following this article, the rumor gained more traction, and it was given an even larger audience when a caller by the name of ‘Tom’ called into Russ Gibb, a radio DJ on WKNR-FM in Michigan and asked to discuss the rumors of McCartney’s death. He pointed Gibb toward a song called ‘Revolution 9’ and told him to play it backwards. “I shoulda brushed the kid off,” recalled Gibb, “He said ‘play the record backwards’ I said ‘what!’” The notion was a strange one at the time, but Gibb dutifully played it backwards and heard the words “number nine, number nine” turn into something different, “When I spun it backwards it said, ‘turn me on dead man, turn me on dead man’. I freaked.” In the music, there are the opening words of ‘Got To Get You Into My Life’ which state: “I was alone, I took a ride, I didn’t know what I would find there”.
